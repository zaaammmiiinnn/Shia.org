Imam Ja'far al-Sadiq (AS)

Imam Ja'far ibn Muhammad al-Sadiq (702-765 CE), the sixth Imam of the Ahl al-Bayt, was a towering intellectual figure whose teachings formed the foundation of Shi'a jurisprudence and theology. Beyond his religious leadership, Imam al-Sadiq was a polymath who made significant contributions to various sciences including chemistry, astronomy, mathematics, and medicine.

Born

17th Rabi' al-Awwal 83 AH (702 CE)

Place of Birth

Medina, Arabia

Title

Al-Sadiq (The Truthful)

Notable Students

Jabir ibn Hayyan, Abu Hanifa, Malik ibn Anas

Life and Legacy

Key events in the life of Imam Ja'far al-Sadiq

Birth

Born in Medina in 702 CE to Imam Muhammad al-Baqir and Umm Farwah. Named Ja'far ("river") and given the title al-Sadiq ("the truthful") for his impeccable character.

Early Education

Received education from his father Imam al-Baqir and grandfather Imam Zayn al-Abidin in religious sciences, philosophy, and natural sciences.

Imamate

Became the sixth Imam after his father's martyrdom in 733 CE. Established the famous teaching circle in Medina that attracted thousands of students.

Scientific Contributions

Between 750-760 CE, Imam al-Sadiq made his most significant scientific contributions through his student Jabir ibn Hayyan, laying foundations for modern chemistry.

Hadith Collection

Compiled extensive collections of hadith and teachings that form the basis of Shi'a jurisprudence. His legal opinions influenced all major Islamic schools of thought.

Martyrdom

Poisoned by order of the Abbasid caliph al-Mansur in 765 CE. Buried in Jannat al-Baqi cemetery in Medina, where his shrine was a place of pilgrimage until its destruction in 1925.
